what was it?

Summary

Summary The Brazilian coffee crisis was a mass state of panic in Brazil during the 1930s when extreme overproduction of coffee left the workers of the Brazilian coffee industry out of a job and out of space to store any more of the already harvested product. Between 1931 and 1944, Brazilian coffee farmers and companies burned a total of 78.2 million bags of exportable coffee. That equates to an astounding 10.3 billion pounds of coffee.

How it started

By 1906 Brazil had begun to take measures to capitalize on its best-selling natural resource. An abundance of coffee bean production leads to an exponential drop in the price of household coffee. To prevent a crash in the coffee industry the government officials of multiple coffee producing states began to buy surplus coffee stocks using international loans to stabilize the market prices. In hindsight this only left coffee producing states more dependent on the coffee industry.

How it happened

Following the roaring twenties, Brazil was heavily reliant on the coffee industry to provide for over 50% of its exported goods. Then came the stock market crash of 1929 which ushered in The Great Depression, a new era where people could no longer afford expensive unroasted coffee beans that needed to be imported from South America. To say the least, Brazilian farmers felt the shock waves of the northern depression. Without the high demand of coffee imports, Brazilian farmers found themselves at a standstill of what to do with all the beans they had worked so hard to procure. Coffee producing companies began to lay off workers and burn the excess beans that they did not have the room to store. Between the times of 1931 and 1944 a total of 10.3 billion pounds of coffee was burned to waste.

About the Author

From an exceedingly early age, Trevor was exposed to the advances of coffee in the modern world. He was fascinated by the rich intoxicating smell of the roasted beans. Trevor described his interests in coffee to really take flight when he first tasted the South African nectar. He was bamboozled about how something could smell so sweet yet tasted so bitter. He described it as a wave of bitterness followed by a raw uninfluenced aftertaste that lingered on the tongue for minutes to come. He later wrote about this sensation in his New York Times best selling blog article, “The Taste of Coffee”, where he described it as nature's best metaphor for life and death. In his later years Trevor continues his search for the perfect cup of joe, this search takes him to all corners of the globe. However, it was not until the end of another journey when his mentor Sir Stogran of Central Memorial High School commanded him to dive into the histories of this symbol of American independence that we call Coffee.
